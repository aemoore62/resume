How I got here

We can automate this with spreadsheets.

Prior to beginning my undergrad education, I worked as a quality assurance professional. I saw an opportunity to automate generating labels by developing an app in MS Excel. Not knowing anything about programming, I started with macros and then discovered that I could directly tell the computer what I want it to do instead of recording what I want it to do. Little did I realize that this project was the starting block for my effort to advance sharing information about scientific experiments.

We can use slime mold to help humans make decisions.

As an undergrad, I collaborated with diverse researchers to explore how slime mold may be used to respond to problems in society. Slime mold are single-celled organisms that ban together when the going gets tough. I used slime mold to investigate the distribution of and access to human food sources. Namely, I mapped the slime mold's food sources with varying levels of nutrients to food stores with different varieties of products, e.g. convenience store vs. super market. I strategically placed slime mold's food over top a map of stores located in Baltimore, Maryland, USA. Then, I filmed how the slime mold navigated the food stores. This project enabled me to see how I can make use of unexpected resources to approach problems in an unconventional fashion.

Why's it so hard to find and use the metadata?

After attending a scientific conference for the first time as an undergrad, I decided that I wanted to use public data to investigate the impact of lung cancer treatments on biological outcomes. Given that my first exposure to scientists was in an FDA-regulated, I assumed that all experiments are conducted in a highly consisten fashion and that significat details about experimentation are reported. I was shocked to learn how common it is for similar studies to be conducted with different material and procedures. I was further shocked to learn how difficult it was to access common, critical metadata like the number of passages for cells. I quickly realized that I couldn't conduct the original study and instead investigated the impact of experimental factors on untreated cells. This project ignited my interest to ease reuse of both metadata and analytical data.

sCubed positions metadata to mutually benefit authors and the scientific community.

As a graduate student, I joined the Edison Lab and the Compound Identification Core (CIDC) in the NIH Metabolomics Consortium. While a primary goal of the CIDC was to advance methods for identifying compounds, the CIDC also had a goal to share complex information about how they were conducting their experiments. The CIDC knew that sharing this info would enable others to reuse the CIDC's research output. In this group, I began developing approaches to managing complex metadata related to heterogenous analytical data. My work in this group founded the user-centric approach that I adopt when developing applications.
